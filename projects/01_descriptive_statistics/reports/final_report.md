## Analytical Framework

Before reviewing any output, the analysis was guided by a predefined checklist built into the pipeline.

This reflects the standard approach used in descriptive analysis:

- Identify variable types (continuous, binary, categorical)  
- Check central tendency (mean vs median)  
- Review spread (quartiles, IQR)  
- Detect outliers (IQR-based thresholds)  
- Identify skew (mean vs median divergence)  
- Flag suspicious values (e.g. round-number caps)  
- Verify completeness (missing values)  

These checks were partially automated in the statistical engine, but also used as a **manual validation framework** when reviewing the results.

## Key Observations

### 1. Price, views, and listing age are all right-skewed

Across both the statistics and the plots, three variables stand out:

- `item_price`  
- `views`  
- `listing_age_days`  

All show **clear right skew**, where a small number of large values pull the mean up.

Example:
- Median price ≈ 24.9  
- Mean price ≈ 32.9  

That gap (~32%) is visible in the histogram as a long right tail.

👉 Conclusion:  
For these variables, **median is a better representation than mean**.

---

### 2. Views distribution is heavily dominated by outliers

The `views` variable shows the strongest skew:

- Median ≈ 30  
- Mean ≈ 46.7  
- Max = 1000  

From the plot:
- most listings cluster at relatively low views  
- a small number spike very high  

There is also a single value at **1000 views**:

- Only **1 record** reaches this value  
- This makes a hard platform cap less likely  
- However, it is still a round-number maximum and should be verified at source level  

👉 Conclusion:  
Engagement is **highly uneven**, and a few listings likely dominate visibility.

---

### 3. Likes behave as expected (good sanity check)

Unlike the other engagement metrics:

- Mean ≈ Median ≈ 4  
- Very low outlier rate  

The histogram is roughly symmetric.

👉 Conclusion:  
This is a **well-behaved variable**, which helps validate that the pipeline is not over-flagging skew everywhere.

---

### 4. Shipping price is clearly tier-based, not continuous

`shipping_price` only takes 5 values:

- 2.49, 2.99, 3.49, 4.99, 5.99  

The distribution is almost perfectly even across them.

From both the stats and the bar chart:
- this is not a natural distribution  
- it’s a **predefined pricing structure**  

👉 Conclusion:  
This should be treated as **categorical/ordinal**, not continuous.

---

### 5. Seller ratings are compressed near the top

- Mean ≈ 4.57  
- Very small spread (IQR ≈ 0.46)  
- Most values are close to 5.0  

The plot shows a clear clustering near the upper bound.

👉 Conclusion:  
This variable likely suffers from a **ceiling effect**, meaning it won’t be very useful for distinguishing between sellers without transformation.

---

### 6. Clear drop-off from cart to purchase

- Add-to-cart rate: **18.9%**  
- Purchase rate: **11.1%**  

From these:

- **Cart to Purchase conversion ≈ 58.7%** (111 / 189)

This indicates:
- not all add-to-cart events lead to purchase  
- there is a measurable drop-off between intent and completion  

👉 Conclusion:  
The conversion gap is now **quantified**, and represents a clear next area for deeper analysis (e.g. funnel breakdown, segmentation).

---

### 7. Category distribution is imbalanced

- Clothing = 34% of listings  
- Electronics = 9%  

This shows up clearly in the bar chart.

👉 Conclusion:  
Any comparison across categories needs to account for this imbalance.
